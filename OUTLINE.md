# I&S G7 — Unit 4 Textbook
## Outline

*A short student textbook for the Information Technology unit. Six story-driven chapters plus a prologue and epilogue. Each chapter is somewhat self-contained but a single narrative through-line runs across all of them.*

---

## Working Titles (pick one)

- **The Pattern: Five Hundred Years of Information Technology**
- **Miracle, Ordinary, Weapon: A Short History of How Information Changed the World**
- **How Information Changed the World**
- **The Stories That Changed Everything**

---

## Audience & Reading Level Notes

- Written for **Grade 7 / MYP Year 2** students (12–13 years old).
- All classes include EAL learners — language must be scaffolded but not dumbed down. Tier 2 academic vocabulary should be used and explicitly defined in sidebars.
- Total length target: **~9,000–10,000 words** (six chapters of ~1,200–1,800 words each, plus prologue and epilogue).
- Tone: narrative, vivid, slightly journalistic. Story first; analysis built in around the story rather than separated from it.

---

## The Through-Line

The whole book builds toward a single transferable idea:

> **Every new information technology starts as a miracle, becomes ordinary, and eventually gets weaponized. The pattern has been running for 500 years.**

By the end of the book, students should be able to:

1. Tell the six stories in their own words.
2. Recognize the **miracle → ordinary → weaponized** pattern in any new technology.
3. Identify the winners and losers of any information-technology shift.
4. Apply the pattern to their own summative video about a technology of their choice.

---

## Standard Chapter Structure

Every chapter follows the same eight-part shape so students learn the rhythm:

1. **The Moment** — opens with a single dramatic scene (the hook). 200–300 words.
2. **Before** — what the world looked like before this technology. 150–250 words.
3. **The Tech** — what the invention actually was, in plain language. Diagrams or images. 150–250 words.
4. **The Ripples** — what changed. Winners and losers. Unintended consequences. 300–400 words.
5. **The Pattern** — explicitly names where on the miracle → ordinary → weaponized arc this story sits, and connects it back to the previous chapter. 150–200 words.
6. **Now** *(deliberately brief and understated in Chapters 1–5)* — A 1–3 sentence quiet closer that hints at the pattern's continuation without naming specific modern technologies (no TikTok, no iPhone, no internet name-checks). The book's connection to the present should build *implicitly* across the early chapters and become fully explicit only in Chapter 6. ~25–60 words in Chapters 1–5; ~100–150 words in Chapter 6.
7. **Sidebar: Key Words** — Tier 2 academic vocabulary defined for EAL learners. 5–8 words per chapter.
8. **Reflect** — 2 questions. One factual, one conceptual or debatable.

Each chapter also includes:

- **A "Pattern Tracker" graphic** at the end — a simple visual showing where on the miracle/ordinary/weapon arc the chapter sits, with previous chapters marked too. Builds across the book.
- **A "Voices from the Time" quote** — one short primary source (a letter, a newspaper headline, a recorded line) to give the era a real voice.
- **Image suggestions** — 2–3 historical images per chapter, listed at the end of the outline so the teacher can source them.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Outline

### Prologue: "The Pattern"

**Purpose:** Set up the question and the through-line. Tell readers what they're about to read and why.

**Contents:**
- Open with a question: *What makes some inventions actually change the world?*
- Introduce the miracle → ordinary → weaponized arc as a "rumor" or "hunch" — not yet proven.
- Promise: in the next six chapters, you'll see this pattern repeat. By the end, you'll spot it on your own.
- Frame the summative: by the end of the unit, you'll make a 60-second video telling one of these stories yourself.

**Approximate length:** ~600 words.

---

### Chapter 1 — A Monk, a Machine, and a Door
*The printing press, 1450s–1500s*

**The Moment:** October 31, 1517. A young priest named Martin Luther nails a piece of paper to a church door in Wittenberg. The paper attacks the sale of **indulgences** — payments people made to the Catholic Church in exchange for reducing punishment for sin. Within two weeks, copies of Luther's argument are being read across Germany. Within two months, across Europe. He has no idea. *(Explain indulgences clearly here — most G7 students will not have encountered the concept.)*

**Before:** A single Bible took a monk a full year to copy by hand. Books were rare. Most people couldn't read — and even if they could, there was almost nothing to read.

**The Tech:** Johannes Gutenberg's movable-type printing press (~1440s). Plain explanation of how it works. One press could make ~3,600 pages a day.

**The Ripples:**
- The **Reformation** — define plainly: a religious movement in which whole regions of Europe broke away from the Catholic Church to form new churches. The word comes from *reform* (to remake / fix). Luther's pamphlets fueled it.
- Vernacular Bibles — ordinary people reading sacred text in their own language for the first time.
- Rising literacy across Europe.
- Censorship pushback — the Catholic Church's *Index of Forbidden Books*.
- Newspapers and coffeehouses (1600s).
- The losers: scribes, the Church's monopoly on knowledge.

**The Pattern:** First full run of the arc. Miracle (1450s–1517). Ordinary (1500s). Weaponized (Reformation pamphlet wars, religious wars).

**Now:** *(brief — ~30 words)* The press is long gone. The pattern it set running is not. Watch for it as we go.

**Voices from the time:** A line from one of Luther's pamphlets, or a printer's mark.

**Key Words:** *indulgences, vernacular, monopoly, pamphlet, Reformation, propaganda, obsolete, distribution.*

**Reflect:**
1. Why did Luther's ideas spread so fast in 1517 when similar ideas had failed to spread in earlier centuries?
2. If the printing press had never been invented, do you think the Reformation would have happened anyway? Why or why not?

**Approximate length:** ~1,500 words.

---

### Chapter 2 — The Two-Thousand-Year Speed Limit
*The telegraph, 1840s–1860s*

**The Moment:** May 24, 1844. Samuel Morse taps out a message in the U.S. Capitol building. Forty miles away, in Baltimore, his assistant receives it almost instantly: *"What hath God wrought?"* It is the first time in human history that information has moved faster than a human body could carry it.

**Before:** For 2,000 years, news traveled at the speed of a horse, a ship, or a runner. Roman emperors got news as fast as Napoleon did — about 30 miles per hour at best.

**The Tech:** Morse code, copper wires, electrical signals. Plain explanation.

**The Ripples:**
- The 1858 transatlantic cable — they laid a wire across the Atlantic Ocean. It worked for three weeks, then died. Eight years later they tried again and succeeded.
- Stock markets, war reporting, weather forecasts, time zones — all transformed.
- "The Victorian Internet" (Tom Standage): everything we say about the internet today was said about the telegraph first.
- The losers: town criers, slow newspapers, anyone whose business depended on information being scarce.

**The Pattern:** Miracle (1840s) → ordinary (1860s) → weaponized (1890s yellow journalism, propaganda telegrams in WWI).

**Now:** *(brief — ~30 words)* We still live with the consequences of what Morse tapped out in 1844. The arc has not stopped.

**Voices from the time:** Queen Victoria's 1858 cable to President Buchanan, or a newspaper headline announcing the cable was working.

**Key Words:** *transatlantic, simultaneous, propaganda, transmit, scarcity, journalism, infrastructure.*

**Reflect:**
1. Why was "What hath God wrought?" such a dramatic first message? What was Morse trying to say about what the telegraph meant?
2. Standage calls the telegraph "the Victorian Internet." What does that comparison help us see — and what does it hide?

**Approximate length:** ~1,300 words.

---

### Chapter 3 — The Ship That Wouldn't Listen
*Radio, 1900s–1940s*

**The Moment:** April 14, 1912. Two radio operators on the *Titanic* tap SOS into the dark as the ship sinks. A nearby ship, the *Californian*, has plenty of time to help. But its single radio operator went to bed an hour earlier and turned the radio off. 1,500 people die.

**Before:** Ships could disappear at sea and no one would know for weeks.

**The Tech:** Marconi's wireless. Radio waves traveling through open air with no wires at all. Plain explanation.

**The Ripples:**
- After the Titanic, governments passed laws requiring 24-hour radio watch on every ship.
- 1920s: radio becomes mass entertainment. Families gather around the set every night.
- 1930s–40s: FDR's Fireside Chats. The first time most Americans had ever *heard* a president's voice.
- 1938: Orson Welles' *War of the Worlds* broadcast. Did people really panic? Or did newspapers (jealous of radio) make up the panic? Both stories are real history.
- The dark side: Hitler's rallies broadcast into every German home. Rwandan radio stations directing genocide in 1994.

**The Pattern:** Full arc again, in 30 years. Miracle (Titanic 1912) → ordinary (1920s) → weaponized (1930s–40s).

**Now:** *(brief — ~35 words)* The radio age never fully ended. It just changed shape. The same questions we are asking now — about a powerful new medium reaching every home — were being asked then.

**Voices from the time:** SOS transcript from the *Titanic*, or an FDR Fireside Chat opening line.

**Key Words:** *broadcast, wireless, propaganda, mass medium, simultaneous, audience, regulation.*

**Reflect:**
1. The *Californian* had a working radio but no one was listening. What does this story tell us about technology and human choices?
2. FDR used radio to comfort Americans. Hitler used radio to organize hatred. Is the radio itself "good" or "bad" — or is the question wrong? Explain.

**Approximate length:** ~1,600 words.

### Chapter 4 — The Camera Picks a President
*Television, 1950s–1990s*

**The Moment:** September 26, 1960. The first televised presidential debate in American history. Richard Nixon, sweaty and exhausted, vs. John F. Kennedy, young and rested. People who listened on the radio thought *Nixon* won. People who watched on TV thought *Kennedy* won. Same words. Different medium. Different verdict.

**Before:** Politicians were known by their voices, their writing, and the rare crowd appearance. Most Americans had never seen their president move.

**The Tech:** Television. Cathode ray tubes, broadcast towers, antennas on every roof. Plain explanation.

**The Ripples:**
- Visual politics. Suddenly *how a politician looked* on screen mattered.
- The Moon landing, July 20, 1969. ~650 million people — about a sixth of humanity — watching the same thing at the same time. The peak of mass-media unity.
- Vietnam, "the living room war." First war fought with nightly TV news. Body counts at the dinner table. Many historians believe TV coverage helped end the war.
- The losers: radio (briefly), and the older idea that political leadership was mostly about ideas and arguments.

**The Pattern:** Miracle (Moon landing) → ordinary (1970s sitcoms, family TV nights) → weaponized (1980s attack ads, partisan cable news in the 1990s).

**Now:** *(brief — ~40 words)* We are still living inside the world television made. Image-driven politics, shared national moments, fragmented news — these were not invented by the internet. They were invented by the camera.

**Voices from the time:** A quote from a Vietnam war correspondent, or a transcript fragment from the Apollo 11 broadcast.

**Key Words:** *medium, broadcast, partisan, unify, fragment, footage, perception.*

**Reflect:**
1. The 1960 debate showed that the medium changed the message. Can you think of a time you saw a video and a written version of the same event and they felt different? What changed?
2. The Moon landing united a sixth of humanity around a single image. Why might it be hard for that to happen again today?

**Approximate length:** ~1,600 words.

---

### Chapter 5 — The Typo Heard Round the World
*The internet, 1969–2000s*

**The Moment:** October 29, 1969. A UCLA student named Charley Kline tries to log into a computer at Stanford by typing "LOGIN." He gets two letters in — "L-O" — and the system crashes. The first message ever sent on the internet was *"lo."*

**Before:** Computers couldn't talk to each other. Each one was an island.

**The Tech:** ARPANET, then in 1989–91 Tim Berners-Lee writes a proposal at CERN for what will become the World Wide Web. His boss calls it "vague but exciting." Plain explanation of packets, links, browsers.

**The Ripples:**
- Email replacing letters.
- Search engines reorganizing how humans find information.
- E-commerce remaking shopping.
- The dot-com boom and bust (1995–2001).
- The losers: encyclopedias, travel agents, video stores, classified ads, eventually whole newspapers.

**The Pattern:** Mostly the **miracle phase**, but the early signs of "ordinary" by the late 1990s. Weaponization is just around the corner — that's the next chapter.

**Now:** *(brief — ~45 words)* The internet has already moved from miracle to ordinary so completely that most of us don't notice it anymore. That, by itself, is the clue. The "weaponized" phase is where the next chapter begins.

**Voices from the time:** Charley Kline's lab notebook entry, or Berners-Lee's original proposal title page.

**Key Words:** *protocol, network, packet, browser, ARPANET, hyperlink, infrastructure.*

**Reflect:**
1. The first internet message was a typo. What does that tell us about how big inventions actually start?
2. Berners-Lee gave the World Wide Web away for free. He could have made billions. Why does that decision matter?

**Approximate length:** ~1,300 words.

---

### Chapter 6 — Revolutions in Your Pocket
*Smartphones and social media, 2007–now*

**The Moment:** Choose one — January 9, 2007 (Steve Jobs unveils the iPhone) **or** Tahrir Square, Cairo, January 2011 (a Facebook page named "We Are All Khaled Said" helps bring millions of Egyptians into the streets, and a 30-year dictator falls in 18 days). The chapter could open with the protest scene and flash back to the iPhone unveiling.

**Before:** 2006. No smartphones. No Twitter as we know it. Facebook restricted to college students. Going online meant sitting at a desk.

**The Tech:** Smartphones (iPhone 2007, Android 2008) + social platforms (Facebook 2004 → public 2006, Twitter 2006, Instagram 2010, TikTok 2016).

**The Ripples:**
- Arab Spring (2010–11). Liberation through social media — the optimistic story.
- The complications: many of those revolutions failed, were followed by worse regimes, or saw the same tools used to surveil citizens.
- The misinformation turn (2016–now). Election interference, conspiracy movements, AI-generated fakes.
- The attention economy. Apps designed to be addictive.
- The winners: a handful of huge platform companies. The losers: local newspapers, attention spans, possibly democracy itself.

**The Pattern:** The arc is **playing out right now, in our lifetime.** Miracle (~2007–2012). Ordinary (now — kids born after 2010 have never lived without it). Weaponized (~2016–now).

**Now:** *(longer — ~100–150 words; this is the one chapter where the present-day connection goes fully explicit)* This is the chapter where the book stops being history and starts being you. By this point readers have seen the miracle → ordinary → weaponized arc five times. Now the question turns: where on the arc do you think we are, in this technology, in your lifetime? What are you watching unfold in real time? Push the reader to apply the pattern themselves — naming TikTok, Instagram, smartphones, AI — and to recognize that they are not just observers of the next chapter of this story. They are inside it.

**Voices from the time:** A tweet from Tahrir Square, or a Steve Jobs quote from the iPhone keynote.

**Key Words:** *platform, algorithm, surveillance, misinformation, viral, addiction, attention economy.*

**Reflect:**
1. The Arab Spring started with hope and ended with mostly disappointment. Does that mean social media "failed"? Or is the question more complicated?
2. The pattern in this book has appeared five times before. Where do you think we are right now in the smartphone era — miracle, ordinary, or weaponized? Defend your answer.

**Approximate length:** ~1,800 words.

---

### Epilogue: "Spotting the Pattern"

**Purpose:** Pay off the through-line. Hand the tools to the students.

**Contents:**
- A two-page side-by-side chart: each chapter's miracle / ordinary / weaponized moments, listed in parallel. The pattern made visual.
- Reflection: now that you've seen the pattern six times, you can spot it anywhere. AI? Self-driving cars? Whatever comes next?
- Set up the summative: *Your job now is to pick a technology and tell its story in 60 seconds. You've read six examples. Now make one of your own.*

**Approximate length:** ~600 words.
