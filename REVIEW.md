# Editorial Review

A single end-to-end pass over the prologue, six chapters, and epilogue, focused on what a teacher, a smart G7 student, or a Thai EAL learner would actually catch.

Findings are organized by severity inside each section. Suggested fixes are short — they are starting points for you to react to, not finished prose.

---

## Headline findings

1. **The "arc keeps shortening" through-line breaks at Chapter 4.** Ch 3 ends with "Press 150 → Telegraph 70 → Radio 30." Ch 4 then says television took ~40 years — *longer than radio* — and frames this as "the arc keeps shortening." Ch 6 doubles down with "150, 70, 30, 40, 30, 15." The 40 is the empirical hiccup. This is the most important editorial decision in the review. See **Continuity #1** below for two options.

2. **One real factual error: Ch 6's "By 2012, most adults on Earth carried a smartphone."** Global smartphone penetration didn't reach a majority of adults until roughly 2017–18. In 2012 it was closer to 15–20% globally. Fix is one sentence.

3. **Two famous-but-contested claims in Ch 4 — Kennedy-Nixon "different verdict" and Vietnam-TV-ended-the-war — currently land as established fact and should be hedged.** Both are real legends that capture something true, but the strict empirical case for each has weakened among historians. G7 doesn't need the historiography, but it does need "many historians believe."

4. **The 1858 cable "732 messages" figure (Ch 2) is contested.** The number comes from a 19th-century engineer's account; most modern cable historians cite a much lower figure or simply describe the cable as barely usable. You flagged this in the brief; my recommendation is to drop the precise number entirely.

5. **Word counts: Ch 2 is the only chapter dramatically over (61% over target).** Ch 5 and Ch 6 are also meaningfully over. Specific cuttable paragraphs identified below.

6. **Pattern Tracker SVG bands don't always match the prose in the same chapter** (Ch 5 starts the miracle band in 1965 but the chapter starts the story in 1969; Ch 6 starts in 2005 but the chapter starts in 2007). Easy fixes.

7. **One internal contradiction in Ch 5: the body says "By 1991, he had built the first browser"; the Key Words sidebar says 1990.** Picking 1990 (Berners-Lee's own dating) is cleanest.

## 1. Factual issues

Listed by chapter, with section + paragraph. Severity: ✱✱✱ = a teacher will get pushback if a smart student looks it up; ✱✱ = arguable / oversimplified; ✱ = minor.

### Chapter 1 — Printing Press

No outright factual errors. The numbers (3,600 pages/day; 8–20 million books by 1500; Luther aged 33; St. Bartholomew's 1572) are all defensible.

### Chapter 2 — Telegraph

**F2.1 ✱✱✱ "732 messages" on the 1858 cable.** *(Ripples, ¶4)*
> "the cable carried 732 messages — including a famous one from Queen Victoria"

The 732 figure originates with Charles Bright's 1898 history and is widely repeated, but modern cable historians (Bill Burns / atlantic-cable.com, the standard reference) note the cable was so degraded from the start that this number conflates retransmissions, test signals, and barely-readable fragments. The Queen's 99-letter message itself took ~17.5 hours to transmit. Most contemporary sources for educators now describe the cable as having carried "fewer than 400 messages, many barely readable" or simply skip the count.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "It worked. For exactly three weeks. The cable struggled from the start — Queen Victoria's 99-letter congratulatory message to President Buchanan took more than seventeen hours to send. Then the cable failed. Eight years later, in 1866, they tried again with a thicker, stronger cable. That one survived."

**F2.2 ✱✱ "The American Civil War (1861–65) was the first major war fought with telegraph lines running behind the front."** *(Ripples, "War" paragraph)*

The Crimean War (1854–56) used the telegraph extensively, including the world's first wartime field telegraph. The defensible claim is that the U.S. Civil War was the first in which a head of state (Lincoln) directed field commanders in real time.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The American Civil War (1861–65) was the first war in which a president — Abraham Lincoln — could send orders to his generals hundreds of miles away while a battle was still being fought. So could politicians. So could lies."

**F2.3 ✱ "Yellow journalism" / Spanish-American War.** *(Ripples, weaponized paragraph)*

Chapter handles this reasonably (the framing is causal but softened). No change needed — the chapter doesn't claim yellow journalism *caused* the war, just played "a major part."

### Chapter 3 — Radio

**F3.1 ✱✱ "Roughly 60 million Americans listened" to FDR's first Fireside Chat.** *(Voices from the Time)*

The U.S. population in March 1933 was ~125 million; about 60% of households had a radio. The 60 million figure is the Library of Congress's high estimate and is widely repeated, but most scholarship suggests an audience of 30–40 million. The claim "probably the largest audience for a single human voice in history up to that night" is fine; the specific number is high.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Tens of millions of Americans listened. It was probably the largest audience for a single human voice in history up to that night."

**F3.2 ✓ War of the Worlds panic** is correctly hedged ("Historians today suspect that much of that 'panic' story was exaggerated…"). This is the modern consensus (W. Joseph Campbell). No change.

**F3.3 ✓ Titanic SOS / Californian asleep.** Both ships' radio behavior is correctly described, including the detail that Phillips also sent CQD (not mentioned, but the simplification to SOS is fine for G7). No change.

**F3.4 ✓ Rwanda RTLM 800,000 / 100 days.** Defensible estimate; widely used.

### Chapter 4 — Television

**F4.1 ✱✱✱ "Radio listeners thought Nixon won, TV viewers thought Kennedy won."** *(Moment, ¶ 7–9)*

This is one of the most-repeated claims in media studies, and historians now treat it as partly myth. The original "evidence" is a small (n≈282) Sindlinger telephone poll with serious methodological problems; David Greenberg, James Druckman, and others have shown the strict empirical case is weak. The story captures something true about the medium, but it's not the open-and-shut case the chapter currently presents.

**Suggested rewrite (Moment ¶8–10):**
> "After the debate, pollsters asked Americans who won. A small survey of people who had only heard the debate on the radio reported that Nixon had done better. The much larger television audience, asked the same question, said Kennedy won.
>
> The story may be partly legend — the radio survey was small and the people who chose radio over TV in 1960 may already have been different kinds of voters. But the legend captured something real."

Or keep the existing hook but add a single hedging line ("The story may be part legend, but it captured something real about the new medium"). G7 doesn't need the historiography; it does need not to assert a contested finding as settled.

**F4.2 ✱✱✱ Vietnam War — "Many historians believe that nightly TV coverage was a major reason American leaders eventually decided they could not continue."** *(Ripples, "living room war" paragraph)*

This is the famous Hallin / *Uncensored War* debate. The empirical record actually shows that TV news became critical of the war only *after* major political elites turned against it, so the causal arrow is contested. The chapter says "many historians believe" which is good hedging, but the framing as the default reading is strong.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Public support for the fighting collapsed. Many Americans came to believe — and many historians still argue — that nightly TV coverage was a major reason. Other historians say television followed the country's mood rather than driving it. Either way, for the first time in human history, a war was being fought in front of an audience that could not be hidden from."

**F4.3 ✓ Moon landing "650 million viewers."** Defensible (NASA's estimate). No change.

### Chapter 5 — Internet

**F5.1 ✱ Berners-Lee 1990 vs 1991 inconsistency.** *(The Tech, ¶4 vs Key Words sidebar)*

Body: *"By 1991, he had built the first browser."*
Sidebar: *"Tim Berners-Lee wrote the first browser in 1990."*

Berners-Lee himself dates the first WorldWideWeb browser to December 1990, on a NeXT machine. The first public website went online in August 1991. CERN released the technology into the public domain on 30 April 1993.

**Suggested rewrite (The Tech, ¶4 final two sentences):**
> "By the end of 1990, he had built the first browser — the program that lets you view a web page. By August 1991, the first website was online. By 1993, anyone with a phone line and a modem could load a page."

(Key Words sidebar already says 1990 — no change there.)

**F5.2 ✓ "lo" — first message Oct 29, 1969.** Defensible. The notebook entry actually reads "talked to SRI, host to host" (Kline's lab notebook) — the chapter caption already correctly says so. ✓

**F5.3 ✓ Email "invented in 1971 by Ray Tomlinson."** Defensible — networked email with the @ symbol.

### Chapter 6 — Smartphones

**F6.1 ✱✱✱ "By 2012, most adults on Earth carried a smartphone."** *(The Tech, ¶ on Android)*

This is the one outright factual error in the book. Smartphone adoption did not reach a majority of adults globally until approximately 2017–18 (Pew / GSMA data). In 2012 the global figure was ~15–20% of adults, much higher in the U.S. (~50%) and much lower elsewhere.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Android, a competing system built by Google, launched in 2008 and quickly took over most of the rest of the market. By 2012, smartphones were standard equipment in wealthy countries. By the late 2010s, most adults on Earth carried one."

**F6.2 ✱✱ "By 2018, false stories on Twitter were spreading, on average, six times faster than true ones, because they were more emotional and more surprising."** *(Ripples, misinformation paragraph)*

The 2018 *Science* paper (Vosoughi, Roy & Aral) found that the most-cascading false stories reached 1,500 people about six times faster than the most-cascading true ones — a more specific claim than "false stories spread six times faster."

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "A 2018 study of millions of tweets found that the most-shared false stories reached 1,500 people about six times faster than the most-shared true ones — because they were more emotional and more surprising. The algorithms did not check whether something was true. They checked whether it kept people scrolling."

**F6.3 ✓ Khaled Said / Wael Ghonim / Tahrir Square dates.** All correct. Said was 28; Ghonim was a Google marketing executive; the page launched mid-2010; March on 25 Jan 2011; Mubarak resigned 11 Feb 2011 (18 days, correctly stated). ✓

**F6.4 ✓ Arab Spring outcomes.** Reasonably handled. Tunisia's democratic backsliding under Kais Saied (2021) is correctly summarized as "kept its new democracy for a while and then quietly lost it." ✓

**F6.5 ✓ Mubarak "ruled for almost thirty years."** 1981–2011 = 29 years 4 months. ✓

## 2. EAL accessibility issues

Listed by chapter. These are the spots where a G7 EAL student in Thailand will stumble: long sentence chains, unflagged Tier 2/3 words, US-coded idioms.

### Chapter 1

**E1.1 — Moment ¶6, long em-dash sentence.**
> "He had no way of knowing that something else — something brand new, something most people had barely heard of — was carrying his words at a speed no idea had ever traveled before."

Long. The triple "something" works in English but is hard to parse aloud.

**Suggested:** "He did not know it yet, but something else was carrying his words. Something brand new. Something most people had barely heard of. And it was moving his words faster than any idea had ever traveled before."

### Chapter 2

**E2.1 — Moment ¶8, "awed exclamation."** "Awed" is Tier 3 for G7.
> "It is an awed exclamation that roughly means…"
**Suggested:** "It is an amazed exclamation that roughly means…"

**E2.2 — Before ¶5, "next town over."** US idiom.
> "Most ordinary people did not know what was happening in the next town over."
**Suggested:** "Most ordinary people did not know what was happening in the next town."

**E2.3 — Ripples, Wall Street / City of London paragraph.** Assumes financial knowledge most G7 students don't have. Also the cuttable paragraph — see word counts below.

### Chapter 3

**E3.1 — Moment ¶1, "three football fields."** Ambiguous in a Thai context (US football vs Premier League football, neither of which is the dominant referent at UWC Thailand).
> "longer than three football fields"
**Suggested:** "about 270 metres long — longer than two and a half football pitches"

**E3.2 — Before ¶1, "ship that sank in deep water often simply never came back."**
Slightly awkward; "never came back" is colloquial for ships.
**Suggested:** "A ship that sank in deep water was often simply never seen again."

**E3.3 — Ripples ¶ on Rwanda, "as if announcing a soccer match."** US idiom (soccer). In a UWC Thailand context "football" is the more natural word.
> "and described it all in cheerful, casual tones, as if announcing a soccer match."
**Suggested:** "as if announcing a football match."

### Chapter 4

**E4.1 — Moment ¶5, "By the standards of the time."** Tier 3 idiom.
> "By the standards of the time, both men do well."
**Suggested:** "For that era of politics, both men do well."

**E4.2 — Ripples, "the body part of the deal."** US idiom ("part of the deal").
> "Television had made the body part of the deal."
**Suggested:** "Television had made how you looked part of how voters judged you."

**E4.3 — Ripples ¶ on Willie Horton ad.** "Racially loaded" — concept correct but may slip past EAL students.
**Suggested:** Keep, but add a half-sentence gloss: "...so racially loaded — designed to play on white voters' fear of Black Americans — and so effective…"

### Chapter 5

**E5.1 — Before ¶3, "the room-sized computers at MIT could not talk to the room-sized computers at Berkeley."** MIT and Berkeley may be unfamiliar to Thai students.
**Suggested:** "the room-sized computers at one American university could not talk to the room-sized computers at another."

**E5.2 — The Tech ¶3, "hopping from one computer to the next like parcels in a postal system."** Strong concrete metaphor. ✓ Keep.

### Chapter 6

**E6.1 — Ripples ¶ on attention economy, awkward syntax.**
> "They made the apps as compulsive as it was legal to make them."
**Suggested:** "They designed the apps to be as compulsive as the law allowed."

**E6.2 — Before, "Strangers in elevators still made eye contact."** Cuttable (see word counts). If kept: "elevators" → "lifts" if you prefer UK English elsewhere; otherwise fine.

**E6.3 — Moment ¶5, "fall to police."**
> "after sharing a video that showed officers selling drugs"
Already plain language. ✓

## 3. Continuity issues

**C1 ✱✱✱ The "arc keeps shortening" claim breaks at Chapter 4.**

- Ch 3 closes with "Press 150 → Telegraph 70 → Radio 30. Each new information technology seems to compress the timeline."
- Ch 4 then says "About 40 years this time — faster than radio, far faster than the printing press. The arc keeps shortening."
- 40 is *not* faster than 30.
- Ch 6 carries the inconsistency forward: "150, then 70, then 30, then 40, then 30, and now barely 15."

A reader who's tracking the through-line will notice. **Two options:**

- **Option A (rewrite the framing in Ch 4):** Acknowledge the bump.
> "About 40 years this time — slightly slower than radio's thirty, but still far faster than print or telegraph. The overall direction keeps shortening, even with the occasional pause."

- **Option B (recompute TV's clock to match the others):** Other chapters measure from the Moment to the weaponized phase. For Ch 4 that's 1960 (Kennedy-Nixon) → 1988 (Willie Horton) = 28 years. The 40-year figure runs from 1950–1990 rather than from the Moment. If you adopt 1960–1988 = ~30 years, the sequence becomes 150, 70, 30, 30, 30(?), 15 — cleaner, but it requires updating the Ch 4 pattern paragraph and the Ch 6 recap.

**Recommendation:** Option A. Honesty about the bump is closer to your "narrative, slightly journalistic" voice than artificially adjusted numbers, and it gives students a useful idea (patterns are real even when not perfectly clean).

**C2 ✱✱ Ch 5's "30 years" claim in Ch 6's compression sequence.**

Ch 6 lists Internet at "30" years in the compression sequence, but Ch 5 explicitly defers the weaponized stage to Ch 6 ("The hammer has not fallen"). So the 30 isn't a full arc — it's miracle-to-ordinary only. The epilogue handles this correctly ("arc continues"); Ch 6's body does not.

**Suggested fix (Ch 6 Pattern paragraph):** "The pattern that took 150 years in Chapter 1 has now run, in this chapter, in roughly 15. (The internet's clock from Chapter 5 is mostly contained inside this chapter's clock.)" — or simply drop the "30" from the sequence and use "150, 70, 30, 40, 15."

**C3 ✓ "As Chapter 5 warned…"** *(Ch 6 Pattern)*

Verified: Ch 5's "Now" section says "the technology that disappears into the background is the one that has the most power." Ch 6's callback "as Chapter 5 warned, that is exactly when an information technology has its most power" matches. ✓ No change.

**C4 ✓ The "from the previous chapter" callback in Ch 4 The Tech.**

> "It used radio waves — the same invisible vibrations of the electromagnetic field from the previous chapter."

Ch 3 does introduce radio waves with this framing. ✓ Clean.

**C5 ✱ The "five times before" claim in Ch 6 Now.**

> "You have just watched the miracle-to-ordinary-to-weaponized pattern run five times, in five different technologies, across five centuries."

Strictly, Ch 5 (Internet) is *not* a full arc in the book's own framing — it's mostly miracle and ordinary. So "five times" is a slight overstatement; "four-and-a-half times" would be more honest but clunky. The book elsewhere handles this honestly. Probably fine as a rhetorical close.
